# Website Mirroring Checklist

This is the handoff section for the SEO specialist or web team.

## Why mirroring matters

Google cross-references the GBP listing against the linked website. When the two agree on service intent, category language, location, NAP, and service catalog, the business becomes a stronger local match. When they disagree, the profile loses trust and relevance.

## Core rule

Do not mirror a messy public GBP service list one label at a time without strategy.

Instead:
1. capture the visible labels
2. group them into canonical service clusters
3. map those clusters to the correct website architecture
4. decide which labels belong on a main page versus a supporting subpage

## Homepage

1. H1 matches the primary category intent and primary city.
2. Meta title reflects the primary category and city.
3. Homepage introduces the main service categories with internal links.
4. Footer NAP matches the GBP exactly.

## Service pages

5. Every important secondary category has a matching page or a clearly planned CREATE page.
6. Every core GBP service cluster links to a live page before publishing, or is held until that page exists.
7. Every service page has unique title, H1, and description.
8. Every service page links back to the homepage and to related services.
9. Use a mirroring matrix with these minimum columns:
   - GBP service cluster
   - visible GBP label
   - canonical website page
   - URL or planned URL
   - EXISTS or CREATE
   - notes on consolidation or duplicate handling
10. Avoid creating duplicate pages just because the public GBP list uses near-identical labels.

## Subservices and support pages

11. When the public GBP service list is long, decide which labels deserve:
   - a canonical main page
   - a subsection on the main page
   - a supporting subservice page
12. Main high-value services should usually have their own canonical page.
13. Thin or overlapping service variants should usually roll into a stronger parent page unless search demand clearly supports separation.

## Schema and entity support

14. LocalBusiness schema exists on the homepage or contact page.
15. Schema uses the exact business name, NAP, hours, and sameAs references that match the GBP.
16. Service schema exists on the supporting service pages where appropriate.

## Navigation and linking

17. Main navigation or a visible services hub exposes the core service set.
18. Footer reinforces services, service areas, and contact details.

## Proof and media

19. Service pages show real business proof, not only generic copy.
20. Images and alt text reinforce service and city relevance where appropriate.
21. Product-worthy services should have supporting imagery the GBP can reuse for products and posts.

## Areas served and geo support

22. If the business serves multiple cities, build location support pages with unique content for the real priority cities.
23. Areas served on the GBP should align with the real operating footprint and the city-page strategy.
24. Link location pages back into the service hubs and contact path.
25. If the GBP area-served list includes neighborhoods or zip codes, make sure the broader city support still exists on the site.

## Verification tests

After implementation:
- search business name plus city and confirm the visible category aligns
- verify key service pages return 200
- validate LocalBusiness schema and service schema
- confirm NAP consistency across website and GBP
- confirm homepage language still matches the chosen primary category
- confirm every row in the mirroring matrix points to a live page or an intentional CREATE path

## Common failure points

- GBP category says one thing, homepage sells another
- service names differ between profile and website with no consolidation logic
- address or phone formatting differs between footer and GBP
- planned service URLs are not live yet
- service pages exist but are too thin to reinforce local relevance
- the profile lists many services, but the website only supports a small subset
